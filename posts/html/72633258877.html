Meningitis vaccinations are required by many colleges and universities.
Many states require students to be vaccinated against meningitis before they can start school. Even if it’s not required, the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommends that students get a booster dose if they were vaccinated more than 5 years ago, and especially if they will be living in dormitories.

More about meningitis >>
Young adults, ages 16-21, are at the highest risk of contracting meningitis.
Although anyone can contract meningitis, young adults between the ages of 16-21 are among the groups at highest risk of contracting this potentially deadly disease.*

Meningitis is a serious bacterial illness. It is spread through the direct exchange of repository secretions, throat secretions or saliva (for example, kissing). It tends to spread more quickly where larger groups of people gather together, such as college students living in dormitories.

Early stages of meningitis are hard to detect. It is easy to confuse the signs and symptoms with the flu. Symptoms may include a sudden onset of fever, a sudden headache and stiff neck. It will often have other symptoms, such as nausea, vomiting, increased sensitivity to light (photophobia), and altered mental status (confusion).

While most people with meningitis recover, it can cause serious complications, such as brain damage, hearing loss and learning disabilities.
The best way to protect yourself is to get vaccinated.
The meningitis vaccine protects against meningococcal disease. The vaccine is given as one dose and creates antibodies, so that if you are exposed to meningitis bacteria, your body will be able to fight off the infection. The vaccine provides long-lasting immunity.
