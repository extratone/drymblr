U.S. failing to protect kids from HPV
The U.S. is failing to protect children from preventable cancers by not vaccinating enough of them against human papillomavirus (HPV), according to a recent report from the President’s Cancer Panel.*

The HPV vaccine is safe and effective, but only 33% of girls and 7% of boys have been fully immunized with all three doses, even though it is recommended by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). Raising vaccination rates can prevent future cases of HPV-related cancer in girls and boys who are alive today.

What you should know
Nearly 80 million people in the U.S.—1 in every 4—are infected with at least one strain of HPV. Most people are able to clear the virus without any ill effects, but persistent infection with certain types of HPV is associated with different types of cancer and conditions.

There are more than 40 different types of HPV that affect men and women. The type of the virus determines what effects it has on the body. Some types cause genital warts. Others can make cells turn cancerous. HPV causes cervical cancer, but it also causes less common cancers of the vulva, vagina, penis, anus, head, and neck.

HPV does not have symptoms. A person could have the disease and not even know it. There’s no sore throat or fever to indicate infection. The first sign of being infected is often the development of genital warts or an abnormal Pap test.

In order to be fully protected, a person must receive all three HPV vaccines in the series over a six month period.
Who should get the vaccination?
The CDC recommends that both girls and boys get the vaccination; specifically:
  • For more protection, it is best to be vaccinated before becoming sexually active. The CDC suggests both boys and girls be vaccinated when 11 or 12 years old.

  • It is safe to vaccinate girls and young women between age 9 and 26.

  • Boys 13 through 21 who have not yet received the vaccine should also get it. The vaccine can be given to males through age 26, as well.

  • Men who have sex with men, or who have weakened immune systems, should get the vaccine through age 26.

  • In certain states, girls and boys within the recommended ages may need to get the HPV vaccine in order to go to school.
Is the vaccine safe?
Studies have found the vaccine to be extremely safe for girls and boys, with no increase in serious side effects. Future HPV vaccines may soon be even more protective than the original shot. While the current vaccines can prevent about 70% of cervical cancers, HPV vaccines under development will protect against 90% of cervical tumors, the report says.*
