U.S. diabetes count exceeds 29 million.
More than 29 million people in the United States have diabetes, up from the previous estimate of 26 million in 2010, according to a report released June 11, 2014 by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.

“These new numbers are alarming and underscore the need for an increased focus on reducing the burden of diabetes in our country,” said Ann Albright, Ph.D., R.D., director of CDC’s Division of Diabetes Translation.

Diabetes is a serious disease that is on the rise in the U.S.
• One in four Americans (29 million) has diabetes.

• More than one in three American adults (86 million adults aged 20 years and older) has pre-diabetes. Without weight loss and moderate physical activity, 15 to 30 percent of people with pre-diabetes will develop type 2 diabetes within five years.

• Non-Hispanic black, Hispanic, and American Indian/Alaska Native adults are about twice as likely to have diagnosed diabetes as non-Hispanic white adults.
What is diabetes and what are the risks?
Diabetes is a disorder of metabolism, the way the body uses digested food for growth and energy. In diabetes, the pancreas either produces little or no insulin, or the body cells do not respond to the insulin that is produced, and as a result, glucose cannot enter the body’s cells.

People with diabetes are at increased risk of serious health complications including vision loss, heart disease, stroke, kidney failure, amputation of toes, feet or legs and premature death.
How to stay healthy if you have diabetes.
Maintaining a healthy weight through diet and exercise, as well as appropriate use of insulin and oral medications to lower blood sugar levels, can help you stay healthy. Another important part of diabetes management is reducing other cardiovascular disease risk factors, such as high blood pressure, high cholesterol and tobacco use.
We can help.
Our nurse practitioners and physician assistants* provide the following preventative diabetes screenings and services** to help monitor diabetes successfully:

• Diabetes screenings - There often are no symptoms of diabetes, therefore a test to check your blood sugar can help identify if you are at risk. According to the American Diabetes Association, you should be screened for diabetes every three years beginning at age 45. This is especially important if you are overweight.
• A1c checks (also called HbA1c or a glycated hemoglobin test) - If you have diabetes, this is an important blood test used to see how well your diabetes is being controlled. People with diabetes should have their A1c checked 2 to 4 times per year.
• Diabetes monitoring - For those diagnosed with Type 1 or Type 2 diabetes, we are your support system in between visits to your primary care provider, ready and able to help manage the self-care component of your disease.
• Weight Management - Maintaining a healthy weight is very important for diabetes prevention and maintenance. We offer a medically based weight loss program and are with you every step of the way, from your initial health assessment to ongoing coaching to keep you on track.

Our nurse practitioners will provide immediate results, explanations and answers to your questions. If you’d like, we can send a summary of your visit to your doctor.

We’re open every day, evenings and weekends. No appointment is necessary. Most insurance is accepted. The services above are available to adults 18 years of age or older.
