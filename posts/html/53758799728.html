Hepatitis A Outbreak
An outbreak of hepatitis A has sickened 113 people in 7 states according to a report by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)**.
What you should know
As of June 20, 2013, an outbreak of acute hepatitis A has been linked to frozen berries and has affected 113 people in seven states: Arizona, California, Colorado, Hawaii, Nevada, New Mexico and Utah.

The majority of affected people reported eating “Townsend Farms Organic Anti-Oxidant Blend” frozen berry and pomegranate mix which they purchased at Costco markets. It is also sold at Harris Teeter stores, but no cases linked to purchases from Harris Teeter have been reported.

What is Hepatitis A?
Hepatitis A is a virus that infects the liver. It is spread primarily through fecal contamination and is highly contagious. It’s a common misconception that it is only a travelers’ disease, but many cases are not linked to travel at all.

If a person eats food, drinks water or touches a surface that has come in contact with infected feces (stool), they are at risk. Hepatitis A can spread easily in places such as day care centers due to diaper changing, restaurants where employees do not wash their hands properly, and nail salons where tools are not cleaned properly.

Common symptoms include fever, fatigue, nausea, vomiting, and abdominal and joint pain. It can take a few weeks for symptoms to appear and some people have no symptoms at all. The virus can be spread during this time, so people may be unaware that they are infecting others.

Symptoms can range in severity from a mild illness lasting a few weeks to a severe illness lasting several months. In most cases, the infection goes away on its own. Rarely, it can be more serious and lead to long-term liver problems. There is no specific treatment for
hepatitis A.

If you have reason to think that you have hepatitis A or have been exposed to it, contact your primary care provider immediately. He or she will ask questions about your symptoms and where you have eaten or traveled. A blood test can confirm if you have hepatitis A or another form of the disease.
How can I protect myself and my family?
The best way to protect against Hepatitis A is to get yourself and your children vaccinated prior to being exposed to the infection. Two doses of the vaccine are needed for lasting protection. These doses will be given at least 6 months apart.
