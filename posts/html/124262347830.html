“No one believed my father was a battering sex abuser. He was
handsome, a corporate president. He was successful, charming, a
man’s man. He wore tailored suits. He played golf. He drank
martinis. He was celebrated at country clubs and knew the first names
of head maitre d’s at the fanciest exclusive restaurants. He was
arrogant and smug the way Bill Cosby is arrogant and smug. He had an
air of superiority and contempt for those who he perceived to be weak
or incapable of rising the way he had risen. He set himself up as the
chief moral arbiter of right and wrong in the same way that Cosby
asserted himself as public moralist on issues of family values and
crime. He was righteous, particularly about honesty. He was obsessed
with honesty. There were many times when he would beat my head against
a wall or whip me with belts for lies he imagined I had told or would
one day possibly tell. I never understood why he was so angry. I think
I do now. He was raging because he was caught between two competing,
demanding personas, one public, one private. He was raging because his
whole life was one big seething contaminating lie. A lie that was
supported and nurtured by the power that men have over women, adoring
and terrified colleagues, and my dependent mother. A lie that got him
countless free passes, second chances, and cheeks turned.

Like my father, Bill Cosby was allegedly one thing in public, Dr.
Huxtable, and another in the dark or behind closed
doors…”
