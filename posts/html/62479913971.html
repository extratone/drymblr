CDC: Measles still a threat in the U.S.
Measles outbreaks are on the rise in communities where high percentages of people are not vaccinated, according to a recent report by the Centers for Disease Control (CDC).

So far this year, 159 cases of measles have been reported in 16 states. Outbreaks often begin when the virus is brought into the country by travelers who visit areas with a high number of unvaccinated people. Therefore, it’s important to make sure that you and your family members are vaccinated.

What are signs and symptoms of measles?
Measles is a contagious infection caused by a virus. Signs and symptoms appear 7 to 14 days after being exposed to the virus. At first, you may have a mild fever, runny nose, sneezing and cough, red, sore eyes or sore throat. After two or three days, a rash will appear. It starts on the face. It will spread over your body over a couple of days. A high fever may also be present.
How are measles spread?
When someone with measles coughs, sneezes or breathes, they are spreading the virus into the air and can infect people around them. It is also spread if you put your fingers in your mouth or nose or rub your eyes after touching an infected surface.
How should I protect myself and my family?
The best protection against measles is the MMR vaccine. It is given in two doses, typically during childhood. Adults should also consider getting vaccinated, especially those who were born after 1956, work in a medical facility or are planning to or may become pregnant.
